## Product Strategy Guide

This source document provides the strategic framework referenced by product management workflows. It defines how product decisions connect to company strategy, how to evaluate strategic alignment, and how to communicate product direction.

### Strategy Hierarchy

Product decisions exist within a hierarchy:

1. **Company Mission** — Why the company exists. Changes rarely.
2. **Company Vision** — Where the company is heading over the next 3-5 years.
3. **Company Strategy** — How the company will get there (market positioning, competitive approach, growth model).
4. **Product Strategy** — How the product contributes to company strategy (which markets, which segments, which problems).
5. **Product Roadmap** — What the product team will build over the next 2-4 quarters to execute the product strategy.
6. **Feature Specifications** — Detailed definitions of individual deliverables on the roadmap.

Every product decision should trace upward through this hierarchy. If a feature cannot be connected to the product strategy, question whether it belongs on the roadmap.

### Strategic Alignment Test

For any proposed initiative, answer:

| Question | Good Answer | Warning Sign |
|----------|------------|--------------|
| Which company OKR does this support? | Specific, named OKR with measurable connection | "It supports growth generally" |
| Which user segment benefits? | Named segment with validated need | "All users" or "potential users" |
| What happens if we don't do this? | Specific, quantified consequence | "We might fall behind" |
| What are we choosing NOT to do? | Named alternatives that were deprioritised | "Nothing — this doesn't conflict with anything" |
| How will we measure success? | Specific metric, target, and timeline | "We'll know it when we see it" |

### Product Strategy Components

**Vision Statement**
A compelling description of the future the product creates. Good vision statements are:
- Aspirational but achievable
- Customer-focused (describes the customer's world, not the product's features)
- Time-bound (implicitly or explicitly)
- Differentiating (could not describe a competitor's product)

**Strategic Pillars**
3-5 focus areas that define where the product team invests. Each pillar should:
- Map to a customer need or market opportunity
- Be measurable (have associated metrics)
- Be durable (last at least 2-3 quarters)
- Be distinct (minimal overlap between pillars)

**Target Segments**
Clearly defined user segments with:
- Demographic or firmographic characteristics
- Jobs to be done (what they are trying to accomplish)
- Current alternatives (how they solve the problem today)
- Willingness to pay (or other value exchange)

### Decision Frameworks

**Build vs Buy vs Partner**
- Build when it's core to your differentiation and you have the capability
- Buy when it's necessary but not differentiating and a good solution exists
- Partner when you need capabilities you cannot build and cannot buy outright

**Now vs Next vs Later**
- Now: High confidence, high impact, resources available, dependencies resolved
- Next: High confidence, high impact, but blocked by a Now item or pending resource
- Later: Lower confidence, needs more validation, or dependent on Now/Next outcomes

### Communication Standards

Product strategy communications should:
- Start with the customer problem, not the solution
- Include both what you are doing and what you are explicitly not doing
- Quantify expected outcomes where possible
- Acknowledge risks and trade-offs honestly
- Be updated quarterly at minimum
